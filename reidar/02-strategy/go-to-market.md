# Go-to-Market Strategy: Reidar
**Phase:** 4 — Strategy
**Project:** reidar
**Date:** 2026-03-29

---

## Launch Strategy Overview

Reidar's GTM is built on a single insight: the 2,500 emerging VC funds in the US are underserved by every existing deal management tool, they are already paying for software, and they make buying decisions unilaterally in 2–6 weeks when the right trigger hits. The go-to-market is not a broad acquisition play — it is a precision beachhead operation. NYC-based Fund I–III GPs are the starting population because they have the highest density outside Silicon Valley, a culture that responds to cold outreach, and active public brand-building goals that make them natural amplifiers. The channel sequence is deliberate: community infiltration before cold outreach, cold outreach before paid media, word-of-mouth engineering before conferences. No channel fires until the one before it has produced at least one reference customer. The Insight Partners relationship is a parallel track — not a funding ask, but a credibility anchor that compresses future sales cycles. The whole architecture is designed to produce 10 paying NYC customers in 6 months, 3 of them publicly referenceable, at near-zero acquisition cost.

## First 100 Customers Plan

### Phase 1: Design Partners (Customers 1–10)

**Who:** NYC-based GPs of Fund I–III ($10M–$150M AUM) who are active on Twitter/X and publicly interested in building their fund's brand. These GPs have a personal incentive to talk about tools they find genuinely useful — their amplification is worth as much as their feedback.

**How to find them:** Pull from three sources simultaneously: (1) LinkedIn search for "General Partner" + "Fund I" or "seed fund" in NYC, cross-referenced against VC Sheet; (2) EVCA and EMC member lists after joining; (3) Twitter/X lists of emerging GPs who post about deal sourcing, portfolio construction, or VC workflow. Build a 200-person master list. First 10 targets are the ones with 2K+ followers and recent tweets about sourcing pain, deal volume, or CRM frustration.

**What you offer:** 90 days of free access, white-glove onboarding (you configure the Gmail + calendar integration personally), and weekly 30-minute feedback calls. Position it as a co-creation relationship, not a beta test. Use the phrase "design partner" explicitly — it signals that their input shapes the product, not just validates it.

**What you ask for in return:** Two things only. Weekly 30-minute feedback call. Agreement to provide a public testimonial after the trial if the product delivers value. Both requirements go into the design partner agreement alongside explicit data privacy terms.

**The aha moment:** During the first week, surface a company they evaluated 3–6 months ago with full context — why they passed, what has changed, what the original email said. That moment, more than any demo, converts a skeptic into an advocate.

**Conversion to paid:** At day 75 (not day 90), send a personal note: "Based on what you've told me, here's what Reidar has done for your workflow over the past two months. We're moving to $500/month after the trial. I'd like you to be one of the first five paying customers." The 15-day buffer gives room to address any final friction before the decision.

### Phase 2: Community-Led Growth (Customers 11–40)

**Which communities:** EVCA (#tools channel, 750+ members), EMC (700+ founding GPs, requires $5M+ first close or GP champion), Partner Track Slack (500–1,000 members). Join all three within the first week. Lurk and contribute for 30 days before mentioning Reidar at all.

**How to show up:** Answer questions about sourcing workflow. Share posts about how VCs actually evaluate companies — not product content, genuine perspective content. The goal in month one is to become a known, trusted voice in the channel before anyone knows you're building something. When the product comes up organically (someone complains about their CRM, about losing track of a company they saw 6 months ago), that is the moment to respond with what you're building and an offer to demo.

**Conversion path:** Community mention → DM offering a 20-minute call ("I'm building something that addresses exactly this — would you spend 20 minutes with me?") → demo → design partner offer or paid trial → convert. At this stage, not everyone gets free 90-day access. Design partner terms apply to the first 10. From customer 11 onward, offer a 30-day free trial with a conversion to $500/month.

**Twitter/X parallel track:** Remi's personal account (not a company account — founder > brand 10:1 on VC Twitter) posts 3x per week. Topics: "Why your CRM doesn't know how you think," "The deal I almost forgot about," "What passive capture means for fund I GPs." The goal is not impressions — it is becoming the person that 50 target GPs associate with the problem Reidar solves.

---

### Phase 3: Referral Loop Ignition (Customers 41–100)

By the time 40 customers are paying, three things are true: there are multiple public testimonials, at least a handful of GPs have publicly mentioned Reidar in community channels, and the product has enough usage data to show concrete before/after stories. This is when the referral program activates.

**Mechanics:** One free month for both the referrer and the new customer. No minimum ARR threshold to participate. Referral links generated in-product — not a separate form.

**Word-of-mouth engineering:** The 5–10 design partners with the largest Twitter/X audiences get a specific ask at the 90-day mark: "Would you be willing to post about what Reidar has changed in your workflow? I'll write a first draft if that's useful." Make it easy. Ghost-write the tweet if needed. One GP with 10K followers posting a genuine take about the product is worth $5K in newsletter ads.

**Momentum trigger:** At 40 customers, pick the one story that best represents the product's promise — "Fund II GP in NYC, 6 months, 3 deals resurfaced, 1 converted to term sheet" — and turn it into a case study. This becomes the anchor asset for every subsequent outbound message.

## The Insight Partners Play (Detailed)

The Insight Partners contact is a warm lever. Use it precisely and in sequence — not as a funding ask, not as a reference yet.

**Step 1 (Week 1–2):** Reach out personally. Frame: "I'm building a tool that learns how each investor thinks about deals from their normal workflow — passive capture, no manual input. I think your team would find it genuinely useful. Would a 20-minute call or a free 30-day trial be interesting?" No pitch deck. No ask for anything except time or access.

**Step 2 (Week 3–6):** Get one analyst or associate using Reidar daily. Not the GP. The person who does the highest-volume screening work. White-glove the setup. Check in weekly. The goal is that they find the product indispensable within 30 days.

**Step 3 (Week 7–10):** If daily usage is happening and they find it valuable, make one ask: "Would you be comfortable if I mentioned that your team has been piloting Reidar? Even informally — 'used by investors at Insight Partners' — changes how emerging GPs evaluate the product." This is worth more than 20 emerging GP pilots in a fundraising narrative.

**Step 4 (Week 10–12):** A single warm intro request. "Do you know 3–5 Fund I–II GPs in NYC who are thinking seriously about their workflow? I'd love an intro — you'd be doing them a favor." One warm intro from Insight compresses 4–6 weeks of cold outreach.

**What not to do:** Do not ask for funding. Do not bring a deck to the first call. Do not pitch Insight as a strategic investor before they have product experience. The sequence is usage first, reference second, intro third, funding conversation fourth (and only if it surfaces naturally).

## Trust and Data Privacy as GTM

The single biggest barrier to "connect your Gmail" from an unknown founder is the VC's fear that their deal flow — which is genuinely proprietary and competitively sensitive — will leak to other funds, train a shared model, or be visible to Reidar's team.

**Lead with the architecture, not the privacy policy.** The first line of the product pitch is: "Your judgment profile is yours. We never see it. It never trains anyone else's model. It lives in your account and your account alone." This is not a compliance statement — it is a design principle that needs to be communicated as such.

**In practice:**
- Design partner agreements include explicit data isolation and deletion terms — send these proactively, before they ask.
- Every outbound DM and demo opens with a data architecture explanation. Not a slide — two sentences.
- SOC 2 Type II certification is a Year 1 priority. Before it is complete, communicate the roadmap: "We are pursuing SOC 2 compliance and will have it by Q3 2026."
- Never use language like "our AI learns from usage" without clarifying "from your usage only, not across customers."
- Offer data deletion on request, in writing, from day one.

The trust gap is also an asymmetric opportunity: if Reidar is the first tool in this category to make data isolation a marketing-forward principle rather than fine-print compliance, it becomes a genuine differentiator against any future competitor who is less careful.

## Success Metrics at 90 Days

**Green (GTM is working):**
- 5+ paying customers at $500/month
- 3+ design partners with active daily usage (Gmail connected, weekly check-ins happening)
- 1 Insight Partners pilot live
- 50+ qualified prospects in pipeline (had a 20-minute call)
- At least 1 public GP mention of Reidar on Twitter/X or in a Slack community

**Yellow (working but slow — investigate):**
- 2–4 paying customers; design partners using but not converting
- Outbound DM reply rate below 10% (rewrite the hook)
- Demo-to-trial conversion below 30% (the data trust explanation is not landing — rebuild the opening)

**Red (GTM is broken — stop and diagnose):**
- 0 paying customers at day 90
- Design partners churning before 90-day trial ends
- No word-of-mouth or referrals from early users
- Insight Partners contact not responding after two follow-ups

The most important leading indicator is not revenue — it is daily active usage among design partners. If GPs are connecting Gmail and using Reidar every day without being prompted, the product-market fit signal is there even if the sales motion is still being refined.

## Fundraising GTM

The seed pitch only works once there are real customers. The sequencing is non-negotiable.

**Step 1 (Months 1–3):** Design partner pilots. Even if unpaid, 5–10 GPs using the product daily is proof of engagement.

**Step 2 (Months 3–5):** First paying customers convert. At least 1 Insight Partners pilot live, even if informal. One GP is willing to take a reference call.

**Step 3 (Month 6):** The pitch becomes: "X emerging funds and investors at Insight Partners use Reidar. Monthly churn is zero. CAC is near-zero because the product spreads through GP networks. Rowspace just raised $50M from Sequoia for the institutional tier at $1M+ ACV. We're building the same thesis for the 2,500 emerging funds they can't serve — at $6K/year."

**Which investors to target:**

- Seed-stage funds whose GPs personally use CRMs and have felt the exact pain — they are the customer and the investor simultaneously. Examples: funds where GPs are known to be active tool users and have written about VC workflow.
- Scout programs at Bessemer, a16z, and First Round — scouts are often emerging GPs themselves; they are both potential customers and potential referrers to the fund.
- VC-adjacent angels: former VCs who have moved to operating roles, people who built B2B SaaS tools for professional services.

**What not to do:** Do not pitch pre-customer. Do not lead with the TAM slide. The pitch that works is "here are 10 VCs who use this every day and here is what it changed for them" — not a market sizing exercise.

## Key GTM Risks

**Risk 1: Data trust barrier kills conversion before demo**
VCs hear "connect your Gmail" and stop responding. Mitigation: lead every cold DM and every demo with the data isolation architecture. Make the privacy terms the first thing they see in the design partner agreement, not the last. Consider publishing a public data policy page before the first outbound campaign launches.

**Risk 2: Co-founder formalization stalls product development during sales momentum**
If the co-founder has not quit their current job by the time the first 5 design partners are onboarded, product velocity becomes a bottleneck at the moment sales is accelerating. Mitigation: establish clear co-founder terms and start date before initiating the design partner program. The worst GTM outcome is promising white-glove onboarding and missing on product commitments.

**Risk 3: Beachhead cohort is too small to generate meaningful word-of-mouth**
Ten customers in a community of 2,500 is not enough to create organic pull. Mitigation: choose the first 10 with disproportionate network influence — GPs who are active in EVCA, who post on Twitter/X, who speak at conferences. The goal is not 10 customers; it is 10 customers who each reach 100 more.
