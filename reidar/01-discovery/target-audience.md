## Primary Persona

**Jordan Mehta — Founding GP**

**Fund profile:** $30M–$80M Fund I or II. Pre-seed or seed stage. 1–2 GPs. No analyst, no associate, no dedicated sourcing team. Every hour of research and every memo is Jordan's.

**Demographics:** Age 32–44. 46% of new fund managers are under 40. Three paths to GP:
- Operator-to-GP (fastest growing — 40% of new GPs now have no prior VC experience, up from 31% in 2022)
- VC Associate-to-GP
- Founder-to-GP

**Goals:**
- Build a differentiated sourcing edge that compounds — not just show up at the same events as every other seed fund
- Return capital for Fund II (the existential objective that shapes every daily decision)
- Build a "founder-friendly" reputation that spreads organically through founder networks
- Create a scalable process without hiring ahead of AUM (no headcount until the fund economics support it)

**Frustrations:**
- Reactive deal flow: events, Twitter, warm intros — all high-volume, low-thesis-fit
- Manual, disconnected pipeline: Notion + Airtable + spreadsheets + Affinity running in parallel, none talking to each other
- 5–15 hours of diligence per opportunity, including memo writing at 11pm after a full day of calls
- No analyst. Every screen, every pass note, every memo: Jordan
- Missing deals: 80+ companies evaluated per investment. The cost of a bad pass is invisible but real
- LP fundraising pressure — every decision is evaluated against "can I defend this to LPs in 3 years?"

**Current tool stack (and monthly cost):**
- Affinity or Notion/Airtable for CRM
- Carta for cap table management
- Crunchbase free tier for market data
- Gmail or Superhuman for email
- ChatGPT or Perplexity for ad hoc research synthesis
- Total: $620–$700/month ($7,500–$8,400/year)

**Representative quote:**
> "I spend more time chasing companies that are probably a bad fit than I spend going deep on the ones that are clearly interesting. The deal flow is there, but finding the right deal flow for my thesis is the real problem. And then when I find something I like, I have to write a 10-page memo myself at 11pm."

**Fund profile:** $75M–$150M Fund II or III. 3–5 person team. Taylor is the one analyst on the team — the sourcing engine, the inbound screener, and often the memo first-drafter.

**Demographics:** Age 24–30. Background via investment banking or consulting, startup operations, or direct from undergrad/MBA.

**Goals:**

- Champion a deal through to investment — the career-defining win
- Build a personal brand within the VC ecosystem as a future GP

**Frustrations:**
- No systematic sourcing tooling — just a spreadsheet and LinkedIn
- Memo writing is 8–12 hours per deal, built from scratch each time

**Representative quote:**

## Anti-Persona

**1. Established Tier-1/2 VC funds ($300M+ AUM)**
These funds have dedicated platform teams, internal engineers, and procurement processes. Sales cycles run 6–18 months. Buying decisions require legal review, security assessments, and multi-stakeholder sign-off. Reidar's founder-led sales motion and flat-fee pricing structure are mismatched for this buyer. Additionally, large funds often build or customize their own tooling — the value proposition of "no analyst" doesn't land with a team that has six analysts.

**2. Corporate VC arms**
Corporate VCs operate under IT and procurement mandates disconnected from deal performance. Strategic objectives (synergy with the parent company) override investment return logic. They are not making ROI decisions based on GP time savings — they are making decisions based on IT vendor approval lists.

**3. Family offices doing direct VC**
Low deal volume (often 1–5 investments per year) means the pain of manual sourcing and memo writing is mild. Relationship-first deal flow means algorithmic sourcing is culturally off-putting. The "AI does the work" framing will read as a threat to their identity, not a solution to their problem. Not worth the CAC.

## Jobs-to-Be-Done Framework

### Functional Jobs (what they are trying to accomplish)
- Find companies that fit my thesis before my competitors see them
- Triage inbound deal flow without spending 2 hours on every deck
- Write a first-pass investment memo in under an hour
- Track where every company is in my pipeline without manually updating a CRM
- Resurface companies I passed on when conditions change (new round, pivot, new signal)
- Maintain "why we passed" institutional memory as the fund grows

### Social Jobs (how they want to be perceived)
- As a founder-friendly GP who moves fast and gives substantive feedback
- As a disciplined, thesis-driven investor — not just spray-and-pray
- As a credible operator-turned-GP who makes bets based on informed conviction
- As the GP who spotted a deal others missed (the sourcing edge narrative for LP updates)

### Emotional Jobs (how they want to feel)
- Confident that they are not missing the deals that matter
- In control of a process that currently feels chaotic and reactive
- Proud of the quality of their diligence, not just the volume
- Less alone — the no-analyst reality is isolating; having a tool that functions like a junior team member reduces the cognitive weight

## Buying Behavior

**Decision process:** Solo or 2-person. No procurement. Jordan is the buyer, the user, and the evaluator. At slightly larger funds ($75M–$150M), Taylor may discover and champion a tool, but the GP makes the final call and controls the budget.

**Evaluation criteria (ranked):**
1. Does it save me real time on sourcing and memo writing?
2. Does it fit my thesis, or does it require significant configuration?
3. Will my team actually use it? (Adoption is a scarred pain point — every GP has a CRM graveyard)
4. Does it integrate with my existing email and data workflow?
5. Is it secure with my deal data?

**Sales cycle length:** Short. Emerging managers are not running procurement. If the tool solves an obvious pain and the price fits the stack budget, the decision can happen in days. Pilot or free-trial formats dramatically accelerate this.

**Common objections:**
- "I'm not sure it will learn my thesis well enough"
- "My deal flow is pretty niche — will this actually surface relevant companies?"
- "I've been burned by CRMs before — is data entry going to kill adoption?"
- "I'm not going to pay $X/month for something my associate (if I had one) would do"
- "What happens to my deal data if I cancel?"

**Purchase triggers:**
- Just closed a fund (Fund I or II) and building the operational stack from scratch
- Recently missed a deal and the pain is fresh
- LP asked for a more systematic sourcing narrative
- Peer GP mentions a tool that's working for them (word-of-mouth is the dominant trigger in this cohort)
- Sees a demo or case study with a fund that has a similar thesis

**Willingness to pay:** Strong evidence at the institutional tier. Affinity commands $2,000–$2,700/user/year despite near-universal UX complaints — GPs pay because the pain is real. Emerging manager total stack budget is $620–$700/month. Recommended Reidar pricing: $500–$1,500/month flat firm-level, tiered by fund size. Pilot pricing at $0 or $299/month to reduce friction on the first conversion.

## Demand Validation

**AI adoption signal:** 85% of dealmakers now use AI for daily tasks (Affinity survey, ~300 respondents), up from 76% year-over-year. 94% plan to integrate AI in some form. This is not a trend to bet on — it has already happened. The question is which product captures the workflow.

**Category emergence:** The "AI investment associate" category is actively being named and defined in 2025–2026. No dominant player has established category ownership. Key rising search queries include: "AI deal sourcing," "AI investment memo," "emerging manager software stack," "VC AI associate." Reidar has an early window to define the category before it consolidates.

**WTP evidence:** The incumbent (Affinity) charges $2,000–$2,700/user/year and retains customers despite persistent, vocal UX complaints. This is the strongest possible WTP signal: people pay for painful tools because the underlying job is essential. A product that solves the same jobs with better UX and AI-native sourcing has a clear pricing and positioning opportunity.

**Market validation score:** High. The pain is documented, the budget exists, the category is emerging, and the incumbent is beatable on experience even at equivalent price.

## Strategic Connections

**Link to competitor-landscape.md:** The pricing sensitivity data above directly validates the $500–$1,500/month flat firm-level pricing range. Affinity's per-seat pricing at $2,000–$2,700/user/year is structurally misaligned with 1–2 GP funds — a flat firm-level model is both more affordable and more intuitive for this buyer. The competitor landscape should be analyzed for: (1) which incumbents have the highest CRM abandonment rates, (2) which tools have the worst UX satisfaction scores, and (3) which players are attempting to own the "AI associate" framing.

**Link to Phase 4 Strategy:** The Jobs-to-Be-Done framework above — particularly the emotional job of "feeling less alone" — suggests that Reidar's positioning should lean into the AI associate metaphor rather than the CRM or sourcing tool metaphor. The product is not a better database. It is a junior team member that never sleeps and always fits the thesis.

**Link to Phase 6 Product:** The pain hierarchy places memo writing and inbound triage at the top. These should be the core wedge features — not pipeline management or CRM replacement. Get Jordan to the first memo draft in under 10 minutes, and the product sells itself.

## Data Gaps

- **Actual churn data on incumbent tools:** We have strong qualitative evidence that GPs abandon CRMs, but no quantitative churn rate for Affinity, Notion, or Airtable in the emerging manager segment.
- **Fund II conversion rate correlation:** No data yet on whether GPs who use systematic sourcing tools have measurably better Fund II raise outcomes. This would be the most powerful ROI narrative if quantifiable.
- **Thesis diversity and configuration complexity:** The research does not surface how long it takes a GP to configure a thesis-based filter in existing tools, or how often they change their thesis between Fund I and Fund II.
- **Mobile usage patterns:** No data on whether emerging GPs are managing deal flow on mobile (which would affect UX prioritization significantly).
- **International emerging manager behavior:** All sourced data appears US-centric. Behavior in EU, UK, and emerging market VC ecosystems may differ materially — especially on tool stack spend and community channel preferences.
