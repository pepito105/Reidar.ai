# Confidence Dashboard: Reidar Research Quality Assessment

**Phase:** 3 — Market Research
**Project:** reidar
**Date:** 2026-03-29

---

## Overview

The research base for Reidar is solid in its structural claims but thinner where it matters most to investors: willingness to pay, actual customer behavior, and the core product hypothesis. The market sizing work rests on a credible Tier 1 foundation — NVCA fund count data combined with independently verifiable competitor pricing — and the regulatory landscape is exceptionally well-sourced, drawing directly from SEC.gov and major law firms. The competitive analysis is among the strongest sections: the conclusion that no competitor has built a personal judgment-capture layer was synthesized from eight competitor profiles and holds up under scrutiny.

The weakness in this research is concentrated in Wave 3 (customers) and the demand signal work. The customer pain data originates primarily from editorial synthesis and a single Affinity survey whose sample is structurally biased toward AI-forward practitioners. There is no primary research: no customer interviews, no willingness-to-pay experiments, no survey data on actual tool spend by emerging fund managers. The $15K–$25K ACV assumption is bottom-up arithmetic, not a number grounded in what buyers have actually paid or said they would pay. This is the most consequential gap heading into strategy.

The biggest information risk going into Phase 4 is the core product hypothesis itself: whether "passive judgment capture" is genuinely useful to a VC, or whether it produces noise that erodes trust faster than it builds value. This has not been tested. Every strategic bet about pricing, positioning, and retention velocity depends on that answer. Alongside it, the 18–24 month competitive window before Affinity ships judgment features is analyst inference with no supporting roadmap disclosure — if that window is 6 months or 36 months, the urgency calculus changes materially.

## Highest Confidence Findings

These claims are backed by Tier 1 sources, multiple corroborating sources, or directly verifiable primary data. They can be used in investor materials without qualification.

1. **2,400–2,600 qualifying US emerging funds** — NVCA 2025 Yearbook (Tier 1) + PitchBook. This is the most reliable number in the entire dataset and anchors all SAM arithmetic.
2. **HBR validates Reidar's core thesis** — HBR (Nov 2025, Tier 1) explicitly frames the AI trajectory as moving from data aggregation to judgment amplification. This is the single strongest third-party endorsement of the product concept.
3. **Gartner: 33% of enterprise apps agentic by 2028** — Authoritative Tier 1 forecast; confirms the macro tailwind Reidar is riding.
4. **Bessemer: 234 analyst hours saved via agentic tools** — Named firm, named report (Bessemer State of AI 2025). Confirms that top-tier institutional investors are already quantifying AI productivity gains.
5. **Reidar classified as software vendor, not investment adviser** — SEC.gov + major law firms confirm the regulatory position. No adviser registration required; compliance path is clean.
6. **SEC withdrew Predictive Analytics Rule (June 2025)** — Direct from SEC.gov. Removes a specific regulatory overhang that was a risk factor for AI-in-finance tools.
7. **EU AI Act + CCPA ADMT timelines** — Tier 1 legal sources with specific effective dates. Directly actionable for product and compliance roadmap.
8. **No competitor has built a personal judgment-capture layer** — Synthesized from 8 competitor profiles. This is the most important competitive finding and it holds across all major players.
9. **Rowspace targets $100B+ AUM only** — Confirmed in company positioning. Directly validates the beachhead: Rowspace is not competing for emerging managers.
10. **PitchBook $20K+/seat, $58K ARPA** — Publicly verifiable. Sets the ceiling on enterprise pricing and confirms there is pricing room below them.

## Lowest Confidence Findings

These claims are based on single sources, inferred data, self-serving sources, or have no primary research backing. Do not anchor strategy or investor projections on these without validation.

1. **18–24 month competitive window before Affinity ships judgment features** — Pure analyst inference. Affinity has disclosed nothing about its roadmap. This window could be 6 months or 3 years. The entire urgency narrative rests on this number, which has no evidentiary basis.
2. **Recommended Reidar pricing $500–$1,500/month** — Derived from competitive benchmarks in an adjacent category. There is no comparable product, no WTP survey, and no customer validation. This range may be completely wrong.
3. **40–50% of micro-funds use spreadsheets** — Directional inference from an Affinity survey plus industry observation. No survey data specifically targeting this segment.
4. **40% of new fund managers have no prior VC experience** — VC Lab claim. VC Lab sells services to emerging managers. This number is structurally likely to be inflated. Treat as directional only.
5. **Generalist funds declined 22% → 5% (2020–2026)** — Same source (VC Lab/Decile Group). Same caveat: the source has commercial interest in the emerging fund specialization narrative.
6. **Typical emerging fund tool stack $7.5K–$8.4K/year** — Bottom-up arithmetic from public pricing pages. No survey data on actual spend. Real spend could be meaningfully higher or lower.
7. **Customer pain quotes from buildingfor.vc** — Tier 3 editorial synthesis. Widely cited but not primary research. Treat as directional color, not validated customer voice.

## Critical Unknowns

These are things we do not know that could materially change strategy. Each represents a decision point that cannot be resolved with secondary research.

**1. Does Affinity's product roadmap include judgment-capture features?**
Why it matters: If Affinity ships this capability within 12 months, the competitive window collapses and the positioning argument breaks. If it's 3+ years away, Reidar has runway to establish a category.
How to find out: Review Affinity's public release notes, product blog, and job postings for signals about AI roadmap priorities. Talk to Affinity customers about requested features. Monitor Affinity's hiring for "AI research" or "knowledge graph" engineering roles.

**2. What do emerging fund managers actually pay for their tool stack?**
Why it matters: The $15K–$25K ACV assumption is arithmetic, not empirical. If actual spend is $5K–$8K, the pricing model needs to change. If it's higher, there is more room to charge.
How to find out: 15–20 discovery calls with emerging fund GPs. Ask directly: "What does your current tool stack cost per year?" This is answerable in two weeks.

**3. Is passive judgment capture actually useful, or is it noise?**
Why it matters: This is the core product hypothesis. If VCs find the captured signals low-quality or intrusive, the retention model breaks and the network-effect flywheel never starts. This is the single highest-priority unknown.
How to find out: Build a lightweight prototype with one or two beta users. Run for 30–60 days. Measure whether they reference the captured judgments in actual deal decisions. Qualitative feedback from the Insight Partners contact is a reasonable starting point even if the fund size is misaligned.

**4. Will emerging managers trust an unknown founder with their deal data?**
Why it matters: Deal flow and portfolio data is competitively sensitive. The trust gap is real and may require enterprise security credentials (SOC 2) before the first paying customer. This affects the funding-to-first-revenue timeline.
How to find out: Ask directly in discovery calls. Offer a data processing agreement template and measure whether it removes the objection. Test whether community credibility (Slack presence, EVCA membership) materially changes the trust response.

**5. How does Reidar price a product category that doesn't exist yet?**
Why it matters: The $500–$1,500/month range is a guess. Pricing a new category wrong at launch creates anchoring problems that are hard to undo. Too cheap signals low value; too expensive loses the emerging manager segment.
How to find out: Run a Van Westendorp WTP exercise in 10–15 discovery calls. Present a feature description (not a demo) and ask the four price sensitivity questions. This requires no working product.

## Research Methodology Notes

Research was conducted across four waves covering market sizing, competitive intelligence, customer voice, and demand signals. Primary data collection was not possible at this stage — all findings derive from secondary and tertiary sources.

**Known source gaps and access limitations:**
- Reddit VC communities were not systematically scraped; community sentiment data is absent
- G2 review data had partial access; reviewer profiles and detailed ratings were not captured
- Several competitor pricing pages are gated or sales-led; PitchBook and some enterprise tools required inference from public sources
- No primary customer interviews were conducted; all customer pain data is editorial synthesis
- Affinity's roadmap is not publicly disclosed; the competitive window estimate is analyst inference

**Source tier definitions used throughout:**
- Tier 1: Primary government sources, Tier 1 research firms (Gartner), named institutional reports (Bessemer), and major law firms with named legal analysis
- Tier 2: Named press coverage (TechCrunch, Fortune), company websites, product pages, PR Newswire, and industry platforms (NVCA Yearbook treated as Tier 1 for fund counts)
- Tier 3: Editorial synthesis, community aggregation, unnamed surveys, and inferred data

The regulatory research is the most reliably sourced section of the entire dataset. The customer and demand research is the weakest.

## Recommended Validation Actions for Phase 8

Ordered by impact × ease. These are the founder's most important near-term actions before committing to a strategy.

**1. Run 15–20 discovery calls with emerging fund GPs (Weeks 1–3)**
Closes the three most important unknowns simultaneously: actual tool spend, trust gap, and willingness to pay. Target: 1–3 GP funds in NYC via EVCA/EMC communities. Ask about current stack cost, biggest workflow frustrations, and reaction to a one-paragraph product description. This is the single highest-ROI validation action.

**2. Test the judgment-capture hypothesis with the Insight Partners contact (Weeks 1–2)**
Even though Insight is larger than the beachhead, a 30-day prototype test with one real VC user will generate qualitative signal on whether passive capture produces useful output or noise. This is answerable cheaply and quickly. The misalignment in fund size is acceptable for a product hypothesis test.

**3. Run a Van Westendorp WTP exercise in discovery calls (Weeks 2–4)**
Add four price sensitivity questions to the discovery call script. No product demo needed — just a one-paragraph feature description. Closes the pricing unknown that currently has no empirical basis.

**4. Monitor Affinity roadmap signals for judgment-capture features (Ongoing)**
Set up alerts on Affinity's engineering blog, release notes, and LinkedIn job postings for roles that would signal AI judgment features in development. This is low-effort and directly narrows the most strategically consequential unknown.

**5. Validate the NYC beachhead density assumption (Week 2)**
Pull actual EVCA and EMC membership lists; count NYC-area emerging managers. Verify whether the community concentration justifies the beachhead choice or whether another city (SF, Boston) has higher density. This is a half-day research task.

**6. Commission a SOC 2 readiness assessment (Month 2)**
Given the trust gap risk identified in unknown #4, understanding the actual cost and timeline to SOC 2 Type 1 will clarify the funding requirement and the earliest date a trust-sensitive enterprise sale becomes possible. The $10K–$20K cost estimate needs a real vendor quote.

**7. Verify Affinity market penetration estimate (Week 3)**
The 18% VC market penetration figure from 6sense is the only number quantifying how much of the target market is already locked into the primary competitor. A more reliable estimate — even directional — matters for calculating the addressable pool. Cross-reference against NVCA fund count and Affinity's 3,000 customer claim directly.
