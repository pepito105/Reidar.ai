# Reidar — User Journey Map
## Persona: Jordan Mehta, Founding GP

**Fund:** Meridian Ventures, Fund I, $40M AUM, NYC
**Sector:** Future of work, labor markets, workforce software
**Background:** 8 years as an operator (COO at two HR tech startups), first-time GP
**Setup:** Solo GP. No analyst. EA 10 hours a week. Deals with 50–80 inbound pitches per month, attends 15–20 founder calls.
**CRM history:** Tried Affinity. Kept it current for six weeks, then stopped. Now uses a Google Sheet he updates less and less often.

---

## Journey Overview

Jordan hears about Reidar through a peer GP in a Slack community he trusts, tries it skeptically because the pitch resonates with a specific pain he's had that week, connects Gmail in 90 seconds, and within 24 hours sees that pitches are being classified without him doing anything. Over the first month, pass reasons accumulate quietly while Jordan goes about his work. At week seven, Reidar surfaces a company — Tempo — that Jordan passed on nine months ago, with a note that references the exact concern he logged when he passed: "team too thin, no go-to-market hire." Tempo just hired a VP of Sales from Workday. Jordan books a call within the hour, shares the alert on Twitter that afternoon, and refers two GP friends to Reidar before the end of the week. The arc from skeptic to evangelist takes 53 days.

## Stage-by-Stage Journey Map

---

### Stage 1 — Discovery
**What Jordan is doing:**
It's a Tuesday morning. Jordan just passed on a company — a workforce analytics startup — after spending three hours researching them over the weekend. Later that day, he sees a message in the #deal-flow channel of a Slack community for emerging managers: a GP he respects, Maya, posts an unprompted thread about a tool called Reidar. She writes: "I passed on a company a year ago. Reidar surfaced it yesterday with the exact reason I passed and told me their new CTO just joined. I was on a call with them this morning." Jordan screenshots the thread.

**What Reidar is doing:**
Nothing. Not yet. But Maya's post is doing the work.

**GP's emotional state:**
Mildly curious, moderately skeptical. Jordan has heard "AI for VCs" pitches before — screeners, sourcing tools, Chrome extensions. Most of them require data entry or produce outputs he doesn't trust. He's not excited, but he's not dismissing it either. He's tired from the weekend research sprint and the resonance is visceral.

**Drop-off risk:**
He screenshots the thread and forgets it for two weeks. This is the most common outcome for discovery-via-referral in B2B SaaS.

**Mitigation:**
Maya's post is personal and specific. It names a company, describes a moment, and reads like a story — not a testimonial. The emotional specificity of "I was on a call with them this morning" is more persuasive than any marketing copy. Reidar should actively encourage this type of post by making the resurfacing moment explicitly shareable (more on this below). The product's best acquisition channel is this exact moment.

---

### Stage 2 — Consideration
**What Jordan is doing:**
Two days later, Jordan visits the Reidar landing page. He reads the pitch. The headline is "Nothing you've ever learned gets lost." He lingers on this. He reads the section about passive capture — no manual logging, no CRM discipline required. He's heard this before, but the framing is different: it's not "we make logging easier," it's "we capture without you logging at all." He looks for the pricing page, sees it's in beta with an application flow, and fills out the form.

**What Reidar is doing:**
The landing page is doing filtering work. The copy is written for someone who has been burned by CRMs. Jordan is that person.

**GP's emotional state:**
Cautious but interested. The "passive capture" claim is the right claim, but he's heard claims before. The trust cost of connecting Gmail is high — not because he doesn't trust the product technically, but because it feels like a commitment. He's weighing "what do I lose if this doesn't work" against "what do I gain if it does."

**Drop-off risk:**
The consideration stage drop-off is almost entirely about trust cost. If Reidar asks for Gmail access on page one, before demonstrating value, Jordan will hesitate and likely leave.

**Mitigation:**
The onboarding flow must lead with the mandate setup — a low-trust, no-OAuth step that lets Jordan see the product working before giving up inbox access. Show him a simulated classification of a sample pitch against his mandate. Let him experience the output before he's asked to connect anything. The Gmail OAuth ask comes after he's seen what it produces.

---

### Stage 3 — Signup / Onboarding
**What Jordan is doing:**
Jordan fills in his mandate: "Workforce tech, labor markets, future of work. Pre-seed to Series A. $500K–$2M checks. US-based but remote-native teams OK. No consumer, no crypto, no hardware." He sets his sector filter, adds a few specific thesis notes in a free-text field: "I care most about distribution-first companies — founders who understand enterprise sales before they build the product." He connects Gmail. He sets his notification preferences: weekly digest on Monday mornings, real-time alerts for companies scoring 8+.

**What Reidar is doing:**
Ingesting the mandate. Pulling the last 90 days of Gmail (with Jordan's permission) to identify pitches already in his inbox. Running classification on 47 emails retroactively. Building the initial company record set. Sending Jordan an onboarding confirmation email that previews what it found: "We've identified 23 companies in your recent inbox. 4 scored 7+. We'll have your full pipeline view ready in 10 minutes."

**GP's emotional state:**
This is the first moment of genuine surprise. He expected to have to do more work. Instead, 90 days of inbox history is already being processed. He feels a small dopamine hit — the product is already earning its access.

**Drop-off risk:**
If onboarding takes more than 10 minutes or feels like setup, Jordan will not finish it. He has a 2pm call.

**Mitigation:**
The mandate form must take under 5 minutes. The Gmail connection must be one click. The post-connection state must show visible progress immediately — a loading indicator with specifics ("Processing 47 emails...") is better than a blank screen. The first result must appear within 10 minutes of connecting Gmail, or Jordan will lose confidence before the first session ends.

---

### Stage 4 — First Week
**What Jordan is doing:**
Over the first week, Jordan gets six new inbound pitches. He doesn't do anything with them directly — he opens his Gmail as usual, but notices that Reidar has already classified them in the pipeline view. Two score 4/10, one scores 6/10, two score 3/10, one scores 8/10. He clicks on the 8/10. It's a company called Movo — workforce scheduling software for distributed teams. The classification note reads: "Strong mandate fit. Distribution-first approach (founder came from Salesforce enterprise sales). Pre-seed, NYC-based. No consumer, no hardware. Weak: no mention of go-to-market stage or initial customer profile." Jordan books a call with the founder that afternoon. He did not research this company. Reidar did.

**What Reidar is doing:**
Running nightly classification on new inbound email. Surfacing the weekly digest on Monday. Watching the pipeline for stage changes.

**GP's emotional state:**
Quietly impressed. The 8/10 company — Movo — is the right call. Jordan would have spent 45 minutes researching it himself before deciding to book a call. He didn't. The product just gave him 45 minutes back. He hasn't posted about it, but he's thinking about it differently now.

**Drop-off risk:**
A false positive — a high-scoring company that is clearly not a fit — will shatter trust immediately. One bad 8/10 in the first week undoes everything.

**Mitigation:**
Classification accuracy in the first week is not a data problem, it's a prompt calibration problem. The mandate setup must be rich enough to prevent obvious mismatches. Reidar should also show the reasoning, not just the score. "Scored 8 because: distribution-first founder, pre-seed, US-based workforce tech" gives Jordan enough to audit the decision and adjust the mandate if it's wrong. Transparency over confidence.

---

### Stage 5 — First Month
**What Jordan is doing:**
Jordan passes on eight companies during the month. Each time he moves a company to Passed in the pipeline, Reidar surfaces the pass reason prompt. He clicks through quickly: "Team" for two, "Market size" for one, "Timing — pre-product" for three, and for two companies he types a short note: "Great founder, but too late in the market — reminded me of [competitor]" and "Interesting problem, wrong business model for enterprise." He doesn't think of this as data entry. It takes him 12 seconds per pass.

He receives three signal alerts. One is noise (a press mention that's basically a press release). Two are genuinely useful: a hiring spike at a portfolio company he's tracking, and a funding round announcement for a company he passed on six months ago (pre-Reidar).

**What Reidar is doing:**
Accumulating pass reasons. Building the judgment profile. Associating pass reasons with mandate attributes. Running signal monitoring nightly for 12 pipeline companies. Sending the Monday digest with a "your week in review" summary of what came in, what was classified, and what moved in the pipeline.

**GP's emotional state:**
The product has become part of the routine. Jordan checks the pipeline view two or three times a week. He's not evangelical about it yet — he hasn't had the moment. But he's not questioning whether to keep using it. It's just there, working.

**Drop-off risk:**
If the first month produces no surprises — no moment where Reidar clearly caught something Jordan would have missed — the product will feel like a moderately better inbox filter. Good enough to keep, not good enough to evangelize.

**Mitigation:**
The signal monitoring must surface at least one genuinely interesting, non-obvious signal in the first month. Not "Company X raised $5M" — Jordan can get that from TechCrunch. Something like: "Company X, which you passed on in January, is hiring aggressively for enterprise sales roles" — a behavioral signal that implies a strategic shift. The pre-meeting brief, if Jordan has calendar connected by now, should also be delivering clear time savings every week.

### Stage 6 — The Aha Moment
**What Jordan is doing:**
It's a Thursday morning, 8:47am. Jordan is on his second coffee, clearing email before a 10am LP call. He sees a notification in his Reidar drawer:

> **Tempo may be worth a second look.**
> You passed 9 months ago: "Team too thin — no go-to-market hire."
> Last week: Tempo hired a VP Sales from Workday (Meredith Hsu, 6 years enterprise).
> Their ARR has grown 3x since your last evaluation. They're still raising.

Jordan stops. He remembers Tempo. He remembers the founder — Priya — and the call they had. He remembers thinking the product was right but the team wasn't complete. He opens the company record. He sees the full timeline: his original classification score (7/10), the pass reason he typed in three weeks ago ("Team too thin — no GTM hire"), three signal alerts from the past nine months that he never saw because they came in before he was using Reidar, and the new signal — the Meredith Hsu hire — with a link to her LinkedIn announcement.

He emails Priya within the hour. She responds the same day. They schedule a call for the following Tuesday.

**What Reidar is doing:**
The resurfacing engine matched the new signal (a specific executive hire in the go-to-market function) against the pass reason for the same company (team too thin, no GTM hire). This is not a coincidence detection — it is a deliberate match between the specific concern the GP logged and the specific signal that addresses it. This is the product working exactly as designed.

**GP's emotional state:**
Stunned, then immediate regret that he almost missed this, then relief that he didn't, then something that feels close to loyalty. The product just caught something for him. Not a generic company recommendation — a specific, personalized, contextual connection that only existed because Jordan used the product for nine weeks. This is not replaceable by any generic tool.

**Drop-off risk:**
None. Jordan is now retained. The question from here is how quickly he becomes an evangelist.

### Stage 7 — Month 3–6
**What Jordan is doing:**
Jordan is using Reidar as his primary deal management layer. He's moved away from the Google Sheet entirely. He uses the pre-meeting brief before every founder call. He's started typing longer pass reason notes because he's noticed that the more specific his notes are, the sharper Reidar's pattern recognition gets. He's using the investor profile view to prepare for LP meetings — he can pull up "what have I been learning about enterprise distribution strategies" and get a synthesized answer from his own decision history.

He starts relying on the AI associate chat. He asks: "What's the most common reason I've passed on HR tech companies in the last six months?" The answer — "underestimating time-to-first-contract in mid-market enterprise" — is something Jordan has noticed but never articulated. Seeing it named changes how he asks diligence questions in his next three calls.

**What Reidar is doing:**
The judgment profile is now rich enough to produce genuinely personalized outputs. Memos are sharper because they're informed by Jordan's specific evaluation history with comparable companies. Chat answers are grounded in actual decisions, not generic LLM knowledge. Signal matching is tighter because the pass reason vocabulary has grown.

**GP's emotional state:**
Dependent — in a healthy way. The product has become cognitive infrastructure. Switching costs are real now, not artificial. Jordan has 47 company records with history, 31 pass reasons, 9 signal alerts acted on. Starting over anywhere else would mean losing all of that.

**What Jordan is doing:**
Jordan posts on Twitter/X. He doesn't post about Reidar directly — he posts about the Tempo moment, because it's a story:

> "Passed on a company 9 months ago because they didn't have a GTM lead.
> This week Reidar told me they just hired a VP Sales from Workday.
> I had a call booked within the hour.
> This is the first tool that's ever made me feel like I have an analyst."

He tags two GP friends in a DM: "Seriously try this before Fund II." Both of them sign up within 48 hours.

**What Reidar is doing:**
The resurfacing alert was designed to be shareable. It is specific. It references the GP's own past decision. It has a narrative structure — a before and after. Jordan didn't post about Reidar because of a referral program. He posted because the moment was genuinely worth sharing.

## The Aha Moment — Deep Dive

**What triggers the resurfacing alert:**
The signal monitoring engine runs nightly. For each company in the Passed stage, it checks for new signals using Brave Search. When a new signal is detected, Reidar runs a matching function: does this signal address the specific concern logged in the pass reason? A new executive hire in the exact function the GP flagged as missing is a match. A funding announcement with no connection to the pass reason is not surfaced as a resurfacing alert — it may appear as a regular signal notification, but it does not trigger the full resurfacing UI.

**What the alert says (exact copy):**

> Subject: Tempo may be worth a second look
>
> You passed on Tempo 9 months ago.
> Your note: "Team too thin — no go-to-market hire."
>
> This week: Tempo hired Meredith Hsu as VP Sales (previously Workday Enterprise, 6 years).
>
> Their metrics since your last look:
> ARR: 3x growth
> Headcount: 8 → 21
> Stage: Still raising (reportedly $3M seed)
>
> [View full company history →]   [Send a note to Priya →]

**What the GP sees when they click through:**
A single-page company view with a timeline: every interaction, every signal, every score, and the pass reason prominently displayed at the top with a visual "match" indicator showing which new signal addresses it. The new signal is highlighted. The founding team section is updated with the new hire. There is a one-click action to re-engage: a draft email to the founder that Reidar pre-writes, referencing the context.

**What action they take:**
Jordan clicks "Send a note to Priya." Reidar has pre-drafted: "Hi Priya, I've been following Tempo since we connected last spring. I saw the news about Meredith joining — congratulations. I'd love to reconnect and hear where things stand. Are you still raising?" Jordan edits two words and sends it.

**What makes this moment shareable:**
The alert is a story with a structure: a past decision, a specific concern, a new development that directly addresses the concern, and a clear next action. Jordan can explain what happened in two sentences. The product didn't just show him a company — it connected a past decision to a present opportunity in a way that no human analyst could have done, because no human analyst would remember the exact pass reason from nine months ago. That specificity is what makes it worth posting about.

## Drop-off Risk Map

**Risk 1: Nothing happens in the first 72 hours (Week 1)**
The product connects to Gmail, runs classification, and delivers the first pipeline view. If the GP checks the pipeline 24 hours after connecting and sees no results, or sees results that seem generic and uncalibrated, they will assume the product doesn't work for their deal flow and move on.
Design decision: Reidar must process at least the last 30 days of Gmail retroactively during onboarding and surface a pipeline view with real results within 10 minutes. The first email or push notification after signup should be specific: "We found 11 companies in your recent inbox. Here's what scored highest against your mandate." Generic onboarding emails kill early retention.

**Risk 2: The first month is quiet (Weeks 2–6)**
After the initial excitement of seeing the product work, the GP settles into routine use. If there are no surprises — no moments where Reidar catches something Jordan would have missed — the product becomes a slightly better inbox filter. Good enough to keep, not good enough to rely on. This is the gap before the Aha Moment arrives.
Design decision: The weekly Monday digest must feel like it was written specifically for Jordan, not generated generically. It should reference specific companies Jordan interacted with that week, name the patterns Reidar is observing in his pass behavior, and flag at least one thing worth paying attention to. Make the digest feel like a smart colleague wrote it, not a report template filled in.

**Risk 3: A false positive damages trust (Any point in Month 1)**
One bad classification — a 9/10 company that is clearly outside the mandate — will cause Jordan to question everything. GPs calibrate quickly; one miss is memorable and will color every subsequent output.
Design decision: Always show reasoning alongside scores. A score without reasoning is an oracle. A score with reasoning is a colleague. When Jordan can audit "scored 8 because: distribution-first, pre-seed, US workforce tech," he can catch errors and update the mandate rather than losing trust in the system. Build in a one-click correction flow: "This score seems wrong" surfaces a prompt to update the mandate or flag the classification, not just discard it.

## Emotional Arc

```
Stage              Emotion             Transition Trigger
---------------------------------------------------------
Discovery          Skeptical           Peer referral with a specific, personal story
Consideration      Cautious            "Passive capture" promise lands differently than "easier logging"
Signup             Curious             Onboarding shows results before asking for full trust
First Week         Quietly impressed   One high-quality 8/10 classification saves 45 minutes of research
First Month        Routine adoption    Product becomes part of the workflow without demanding attention
Aha Moment         Stunned → Loyal     Resurfacing connects a specific past decision to a present opportunity
Month 3–6          Dependent           The knowledge base is rich enough that switching costs are real
Advocacy           Evangelical         The Aha Moment is a story worth telling, and Reidar makes it easy to tell
```

The transition from cautious to curious happens at the mandate setup step — when Jordan sees the product doing something useful before he's given it his most sensitive access. The transition from impressed to routine is gradual and healthy — not every interaction needs to be magical. The transition from routine to loyal happens exactly once, at the Aha Moment, and it is irreversible. The product must be designed around ensuring that moment happens for every user within the first 60 days. Everything before it is the bridge. Everything after it is the moat.
