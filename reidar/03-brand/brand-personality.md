# Reidar — Brand Personality

---

## Brand Archetype Analysis

**Primary archetype: The Sage**

The Sage archetype is defined by the pursuit of truth, the accumulation of wisdom, and the belief that knowledge — correctly understood and applied — is the most valuable thing one can possess. Sage brands are trusted because they make people smarter, not because they entertain or inspire.

Reidar is a Sage brand. The entire product thesis rests on a single intellectual conviction: that a founding GP's accumulated judgment is the most differentiated asset in venture, and that most tools actively destroy it by treating knowledge as shared, disposable, or replaceable. Reidar's value proposition isn't better data or better automation. It's the preservation and compounding of individual wisdom.

This maps cleanly to how the best Sage brands operate. They don't hype. They demonstrate. They speak with quiet authority, as though the truth of what they're saying is self-evident — because to anyone paying attention, it is. They are the advisor in the room who says less than everyone else and is always right.

There's a secondary influence from the **Caregiver** archetype — specifically in how Reidar relates to the GP's anxiety about loss. The founding GP is carrying enormous personal risk. They're managing relationships, capital, and reputation simultaneously, often alone. Reidar's "nothing you've ever learned gets lost" positioning has a protective quality that reflects Caregiver energy: we hold this for you so you don't have to.

**Archetype to consciously avoid: The Magician**

The Magician archetype promises transformation — a before and after, a moment where everything changes, reality reshaped by a new power. This is the language of "game-changing," "revolutionary," "the future of X."

It would be a serious mistake for Reidar to lean Magician. Here's why: the target customer has been burned by Magician-coded products. Every VC tool they've tried promised transformation and delivered complexity. The Magician archetype triggers skepticism in this audience because they've learned, from experience, that transformation claims are how bad tools get sold. Reidar must earn trust, not borrow it. The Sage earns it through demonstrated insight. The Magician borrows it from promised futures that never arrive.

## Emotional Attributes

**First hearing about Reidar**
The emotional target is *recognition*, not excitement. The founding GP who hears about Reidar for the first time should feel a low-grade click of "yes, that's the problem." Not a spike of enthusiasm — GPs are trained to be skeptical of spikes. The moment we're aiming for is when someone says "I've thought about this exact thing" or "I've lost deals exactly this way." Recognition before curiosity. Curiosity before consideration.

**First 10 minutes using it**
*Calm competence*. The onboarding experience should feel like handing something to someone capable and watching them get it immediately. Not wow. Not delight. Just — this thing works and it's clearly been thought through. The GP should feel like they're dealing with a product built by people who understand their workflow, not a team that watched YouTube videos about venture capital. The absence of friction is the emotion.

**After 6 months**
*Quiet confidence*. By month six, Reidar has learned something genuinely private: this GP's pattern of judgment, their recurring hesitations, the thesis language they return to. The GP should feel, at this point, that Reidar is an asset they wouldn't want a competitor to have. Not reliant on it — more acute because of it. The analogy is a good analyst who's been with you long enough to anticipate the question before you ask it.

**When they refer a colleague**
*Selective pride*. GPs who refer Reidar to a peer should feel like they're sharing something valuable, not selling something. The referral should carry a slight undercurrent of "I want you to have this because I trust your judgment" — not "you should try this cool tool." This is the same way GPs refer great lawyers or operators. The product has to be good enough that recommending it feels like a reflection of the referrer's taste.

## Visual Direction Suggestions

**Overall feeling**
Reidar should feel like the best-designed document in a deal folder. Not a tech product. Not a startup. Something closer to a well-produced research report or a high-quality financial terminal — the aesthetic of serious work done carefully. If Granola's success came from feeling like a tool built by someone who respects the workflow, Reidar should feel like a tool built by someone who respects *the work itself*.

**Color temperature**
Cool and restrained. Not cold — there should be warmth in the text and in the copy, not in the palette. Neutral backgrounds, near-white or very light gray. Deep ink tones for primary text. A single controlled accent color — deep teal, slate blue, or aged amber — used sparingly and intentionally, the way a well-designed annual report uses color: to direct attention, not to create mood.

**Typography direction**
Two registers. A serious serif for headlines and moments of emphasis — something with editorial authority, the kind of typeface that appears on the front page of the FT or in a first-edition book. A clean, legible sans-serif for body text and interface copy — tight, high contrast, no rounded corners. The combination should feel like a newspaper that knows it's a newspaper: authoritative without being stiff.

**What would make someone say "that looks like Reidar"**
Dense information rendered with exceptional clarity. Numbers that look important. Whitespace that feels earned, not decorative. A timeline or card that shows exactly what changed and when. The sense that nothing on the screen is there by accident. It should look like it was designed by a product team that had strong opinions about what should and shouldn't be shown.

**What it should never look like**
Gradient backgrounds. Rounded hero illustrations with abstract blobs. Anything resembling a CRM dashboard from 2018. Emoji in the product UI. The Affinity purple-and-white enterprise aesthetic. Stock photos of people shaking hands in front of whiteboards. Anything that looks like a Series A SaaS company's homepage.

## Competitive Feel Comparison

| Competitor | Their brand feel | Reidar's contrasting feel |
|---|---|---|
| **Affinity** | Corporate | Personal |
| **Harmonic** | Encyclopedic | Curated |
| **Granola** | Friendly | Precise |
| **Notion** | Flexible | Opinionated |

**Affinity** is built for enterprise relationship management — it feels like software your firm's COO bought. Reidar feels like something the GP chose themselves.

**Harmonic** is a data warehouse with a VC interface. Its brand feel is the feeling of standing in front of a very large shelf. Reidar doesn't give you more information — it gives you the right information, because it knows what you're actually looking for.

**Granola** has earned real trust in the VC community precisely because it doesn't feel like VC software. It's friendly, clean, and respectful of the workflow. Reidar shares the workflow-respect but goes a step further into precision — less warm, more sharp. If Granola is the tool you'd use in any meeting, Reidar is the one you'd use when the stakes are high.

**Notion** is infinitely configurable, which is its strength and its failure mode. Its brand feel is possibility — the blank canvas. Reidar is the opposite. We have opinions. We've made decisions about what matters. Reidar should never feel like a blank canvas. It should feel like someone already did the thinking.

## Brand Anti-Patterns

**1. Never claim transformation.**
Reidar should never use language that implies it will change how venture capital works, transform the industry, or reinvent anything. GPs have heard this from every tool they've ever tried. The claim activates skepticism. Reidar's value is compounding and preservation — incremental, accumulative, deeply personal. That's the promise. Stick to it.

**2. Never use the word "powerful."**
"Powerful AI," "powerful insights," "powerful search" — these phrases have been depleted of meaning by years of misuse. They're signals that a product doesn't have something more specific to say about itself. If Reidar is effective, we describe exactly what it does and let the reader conclude it's powerful.

**3. Never speak to a room.**
All Reidar copy should be written as though one person is reading it. "Teams can..." and "firms using Reidar..." and "investors who..." are not Reidar's register. "You passed on this company 14 months ago" is. The product is personal intelligence, and the brand should be too.

**4. Never manufacture urgency.**
"Don't miss out." "Limited spots." "Join thousands of investors." This is the register of a B2C growth campaign. Founding GPs at emerging funds are sophisticated buyers. Pressure tactics read as insecurity. If Reidar is worth using, the case for using it should be self-evident from a clear explanation of what it does. The only urgency that's real is the compounding argument: the sooner you start, the more Reidar learns.

**5. Never use the rocket ship, lightbulb, or brain emoji in product or marketing.**
These are the visual shorthand of a certain kind of tech optimism that has become associated, in the minds of many experienced investors, with products that overpromise. The rocket ship in particular. Reidar's visual language should be austere — if we use any imagery or iconography, it should be precise and purposeful, not cheerful. The emotion we're creating is quiet confidence, not enthusiasm.
