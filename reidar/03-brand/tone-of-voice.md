# Reidar — Tone of Voice

---

## Brand Personality Traits

**Precise**
We say what we mean. Every word in a Reidar interface, email, or alert has earned its place. If an insight isn't actionable, we don't surface it. If a sentence can be cut without losing meaning, we cut it. Precision isn't minimalism for its own sake — it's respect for the reader's time and intelligence.

**Grounded**
We don't traffic in hype. Reidar exists because real investors described a real problem in plain, frustrated language: clunky tools, databases nobody trusts, judgment that evaporates between deals. We came from that problem, not from a pitch deck. Our language reflects that. We cite what's true. We don't promise what we can't deliver.

**Confident without posturing**
Reidar has a point of view. We believe shared intelligence commoditizes alpha. We believe the judgment that lives in a GP's head is an asset worth protecting. We say this directly — but we don't shout it. The confidence comes from the idea being correct, not from saying it louder.

**Collegial**
This is a tool made for serious investors by people who respect serious work. The register is peer-to-peer: how a sharp analyst might brief a GP they respect. Not formal. Not casual. The kind of clarity you'd find in a well-written investment memo — direct, intelligent, no filler.

## Voice Principles

**1. Say the real thing.**

We are: "You passed on this company 8 months ago. The thesis has shifted."
We are not: "We noticed you may have previously interacted with a company that matches your updated criteria!"

The real thing is always shorter and more useful. Over-hedging signals a lack of confidence in the message itself.

---

**2. Respect what the GP already knows.**

We are: "Here's what changed since you last looked."
We are not: "As you may know, the VC market can be unpredictable..."

GPs are experts. They've been doing this longer than most software has existed. We don't explain VC to VCs. We give them better information, faster.

---

**3. No filler credibility.**

We are: "Trained on your notes, your passed deals, your thesis — not a shared model."
We are not: "Leveraging cutting-edge AI to deliver best-in-class investment intelligence."

Every generic claim is a missed opportunity to say something true. If we can't say specifically why something matters to this investor, we shouldn't say it.

---

**4. Earn the emotion, don't manufacture it.**

We are: "Six months from now, you'll remember every company you almost funded."
We are not: "Reidar is transforming the future of venture capital!"

The emotion in our copy should come from the truth of the situation — not from adjective inflation. The founding GP who woke up at 3am realizing they passed on the next great company doesn't need us to tell them that's painful. They know. We acknowledge it, we don't dramatize it.

---

**5. Write for one person.**

We are: "You passed on this 14 months ago. Here's why it might be worth a second look."
We are not: "Reidar helps investors surface missed opportunities across their portfolios."

Everything Reidar does is personal. The copy should feel personal too. When in doubt, write for one GP sitting alone at their desk at 7pm — not for a product screenshot on a website.

## Writing Samples

**1. Homepage headline**

> Your judgment compounds. Most tools let it evaporate.
>
> Reidar is the AI investment associate that learns how you think — from the deals you take, the ones you pass, and everything you reason through in between.

---

**2. Onboarding email (first email after signup)**

Subject: You're in. Here's what happens next.

Reidar doesn't do much in week one. That's intentional.

The first thing we do is listen — to your notes, your passed deals, your thesis language, the way you describe a category you believe in. We're not running you through a setup wizard. We're building a model of how *you* think.

By week three, you'll start to see it. A company resurfaces that you'd almost forgotten. A pattern shows up across three deals you thought were unrelated. A new inbound matches language from a memo you wrote six months ago.

That's when it clicks.

For now: connect your inbox, add your last few passed deals, and write a few notes in your normal voice. Reidar will take it from there.

— The Reidar team

---

**3. In-product alert (resurfacing moment)**

> **Monarch Systems** — you passed 11 months ago.
>
> At the time: too early, no enterprise traction. Since then: $4.2M seed from Founders Fund, two Fortune 500 pilots announced.
>
> Your thesis note from November: *"Worth watching if they can crack the sales motion."*
>
> They appear to be cracking it.

---

**4. Pricing page one-liner (Growth tier)**

> Growth is for the GP who's moved past setup and wants Reidar thinking ahead — deeper research, longer memory, more signals, fewer surprises.

---

**5. Founder tweet on VC intelligence**

> The best VCs I've talked to aren't worried about missing deals.
>
> They're worried about forgetting what they've learned.
>
> There's a difference. The first is a sourcing problem. The second is a judgment problem.
>
> Most tools are built for the first one.

## Vocabulary Guide

| We say | We don't say |
|---|---|
| AI investment associate | AI tool (too generic — erases the positioning) |
| Your judgment | Your data (data is what Affinity sells; judgment is what we protect) |
| Compounds | Scales (scales is startup speak; compounds is how GPs actually think) |
| Resurface | Rediscover (resurface is precise; rediscover is soft and vague) |
| Passed deals | Missed deals (passed is neutral and accurate; missed implies failure) |
| Personal intelligence | Shared model (the contrast is the point) |
| Investment associate | Assistant (associate implies expertise and agency; assistant implies task execution) |
| Learn how you think | Train on your data (learning implies understanding; training implies mechanical ingestion) |
| Signal | Alert (signal has investment meaning; alert sounds like a notification from a fitness app) |
| Thesis | Criteria (thesis is the GP's language; criteria is the enterprise software language) |
| Mandate | Parameters (same as above) |
| Portfolio company | Portco is fine in product; portfolio company in formal contexts) |
| Nothing gets lost | We capture everything (loss-framing resonates; comprehensiveness sounds like a database pitch) |
| Second look | Reconsider (second look is active and specific; reconsider is vague and implies error) |
| Deal flow | Pipeline (deal flow is how GPs talk; pipeline is how CRM companies talk) |
| The more you use it, the sharper it gets | Our AI improves over time (the first is a promise about *their* experience; the second is a feature claim) |
